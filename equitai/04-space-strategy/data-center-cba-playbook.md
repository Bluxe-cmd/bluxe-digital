## What Is a CBA?

A **Community Benefit Agreement (CBA)** is a legally binding contract between a developer (or property owner) and community organizations that specifies community benefits the developer will provide in exchange for community support of the project.

**In the data center context, CBAs typically include:**
- Local hiring commitments
- Workforce training investments
- Community grants/donations
- Environmental mitigation
- Tax stabilization agreements

**EquitAI's play:** Position as the **workforce training partner** that helps data center developers meet their CBA training obligations — turning CBA requirements into EquitAI contracts.

## Maryland Data Center Market Overview

### Why Maryland?

Maryland is one of the top 5 data center markets in the US. Key facts:
- **Northern Virginia spillover:** DC area growth is pushing into Prince George's, Charles, and Montgomery Counties
- **Tax incentives:** Maryland offers data center tax exemptions (sales tax on equipment), which attract developers
- **Proximity to federal government:** Defense and federal data centers expanding in MD
- **Fiber infrastructure:** Existing connectivity along I-270 and I-95 corridors

### Target Geographies
1. **Prince George's County** — Largest concentration of new data center development; active CBA discussions
2. **Charles County** — Growing rapidly; Waldorf/La Plata corridor; less organized community opposition = CBA opportunity
3. **Montgomery County** — I-270 corridor; more established developer relationships
4. **Anne Arundel County** — BWI corridor; emerging

## CBA Negotiation Playbook

### Stage 1: Intelligence (Weeks 1–2)
**Goal:** Identify the right project and the right contact

1. Find the project (public notice, news, community tip)
2. Identify the developer entity (LLC name, parent company)
3. Find community relations or government affairs contact at the developer
4. Identify existing community organizations in the project area
5. Determine: Is there already a CBA process underway? Who is leading it?

**Key Question:** "Is EquitAI joining an existing CBA negotiation, or initiating one?"

---

### Stage 2: Community Alliance (Weeks 2–3)
**Goal:** Don't go in alone — build a community coalition

**Why this matters:**
- CBAs are stronger when community organizations are unified
- Developers respond to organized community, not individual asks
- Funders and government support community-led CBAs

**Who to partner with:**
- NAACP local chapter
- Community Action Agencies
- Faith community network
- Library system
- Community college (as training partner)

**How to build the coalition:**
1. Call community organizations in the project area
2. Share EquitAI's CBA framework
3. Ask: "Would you co-sign a workforce training commitment request?"
4. Draft a joint letter of community interest (template below)

---

### Stage 3: Developer Engagement (Weeks 3–4)
**Goal:** Initial meeting with developer's community relations team

**How to initiate:**
- Email community affairs or government relations contact
- Copy local county council member (signals you're organized and connected)
- Request a 30-minute informational meeting

**Email Template:**
> Subject: Community Workforce Partnership Inquiry — [Project Name/Location]
>
> Dear [Name],
>
> I'm the founder of EquitAI, a Maryland-based AI literacy workforce organization, and I'm reaching out regarding [developer name]'s planned development in [county/location].
>
> I represent a coalition of [list organizations] who are supportive of economic development in our community and are interested in exploring how [developer name]'s investment can create meaningful workforce pathways for local residents.
>
> Specifically, our coalition is interested in discussing: (1) a workforce training commitment as part of any community benefit agreement; (2) local hiring targets for construction and operational jobs; and (3) an ongoing community investment fund.
>
> Would you be available for a 30-minute call in the next two weeks to discuss? We're committed to making this development a win for both [developer name] and our community.
>
> Best,  
> Bianca [Last Name]  
> Founder, EquitAI  
> [phone] | [email]

### Stage 4: CBA Scope Negotiation (Weeks 4–8)
**Goal:** Agree on the components and amounts of the CBA

**EquitAI's Core Ask (Workforce Training Component):**

| Item | Target | Floor |
|------|--------|-------|
| Annual training investment | $200,000/year | $75,000/year |
| Training slots per year | 50 participants | 20 participants |
| Contract term | 5 years | 3 years |
| Apprenticeship commitments | 10 slots/year | 5 slots/year |
| Total CBA value (5 years) | $1,000,000 | $225,000 |

**Other CBA components to request (with coalition):**
- Local hiring preference (50% local labor target for construction)
- Community investment fund ($X/year to community grants)
- Environmental commitments (water, power, noise)
- Tax stability agreement (limits rate increases for 10 years)
- Annual community advisory meeting

**Negotiation Tips:**
1. Lead with partnership, not confrontation — developers respond to "how can this work for everyone"
2. Come with data — AI job openings in the county, local unemployment rates
3. Have a "comparable" — point to CBAs in other jurisdictions with similar terms
4. Know your BATNA (best alternative to negotiated agreement) — other funding sources if CBA fails
5. Get everything in writing — term sheets first, then full contract

---

### Stage 5: CBA Contract Drafting (Weeks 8–10)
**Goal:** Formal legal agreement

**Key contract elements:**
1. Parties (developer entity + EquitAI + any coalition organizations)
2. Project description (specific data center project)
3. Training commitment:
   - Annual dollar amount
   - Number of participants per year
   - Curriculum description (reference EquitAI tracks)
   - Credential output
   - Timeline (start date, reporting dates)
4. Apprenticeship pipeline:
   - Number of slots per year
   - Wage floor
   - Duration of apprenticeship
5. Reporting and accountability:
   - Quarterly progress reports
   - Annual audit right
   - Community advisory council review
6. Term and renewal
7. Default and remedy provisions
8. Dispute resolution
9. Governing law: Maryland
